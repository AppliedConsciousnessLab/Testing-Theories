# Systems Science Overview
© Applied Consciousness Lab (ACL)

Systems science studies how complex systems behave, adapt, stabilize, and collapse.

Rather than examining isolated components, systems science focuses on interactions
between components and the patterns that emerge from those interactions.

Examples of systems studied in this field include:

• ecosystems  
• economic systems  
• social institutions  
• infrastructure networks  
• biological systems  
• technological systems  

These systems often exhibit nonlinear behavior where small changes can produce
large-scale effects.

---

# Core Concepts

## Feedback Loops

Systems often contain reinforcing or stabilizing feedback loops.

Positive feedback loops amplify change.

Negative feedback loops stabilize systems.

Understanding feedback loops is essential when analyzing systemic stability.

---

## Nonlinear Dynamics

Many systems behave nonlinearly, meaning outputs are not proportional
to inputs.

Small disturbances may have little effect until a threshold is reached,
at which point rapid systemic shifts can occur.

---

## Tipping Points

Complex systems may appear stable until critical thresholds are crossed.

Once a tipping point is reached, the system may rapidly transition
to a new state.

Examples include:

• ecological collapse  
• financial crises  
• institutional breakdown  
• social unrest  

---

## Network Dynamics

Many systems operate as networks of interconnected actors.

Changes in one node of a network may propagate across the system,
creating cascading effects.

Network structures influence how quickly information, behavior,
or instability spreads.
